# Analytical Thinking

## Initial Observation

Post-COVID recovery is often discussed through headline GDP rebound. That framing is useful, but incomplete. A country can show visible growth recovery while also facing inflation pressure that changes how strong or sustainable that recovery really is.

That became the starting point of this project. Instead of treating GDP rebound as a complete recovery signal, I wanted to test whether the story changed once inflation was evaluated alongside it.

## Core Analytical Question

How does post-COVID GDP rebound change in meaning when inflation is considered at the same time across selected major economies?

This project was designed as a comparative descriptive analysis rather than a causal economic model. The goal was not to prove why recovery patterns happened, but to examine whether growth-only interpretation might overstate recovery quality.

## Why GDP Alone Was Not Enough

GDP growth is useful for showing contraction and rebound. It captures whether economic output fell sharply and whether it later recovered. But GDP alone does not show whether that rebound occurred in a relatively balanced environment or under rising macroeconomic pressure.

A country can look strong on recovery headlines if GDP growth rebounds quickly, but that interpretation becomes less convincing if inflation rises sharply during the same period. For that reason, GDP was treated as a rebound signal, not a complete measure of recovery quality.

## Why Inflation Was Added

Inflation was added as a second lens to improve how recovery was interpreted. It was not used as a complete definition of economic stability, but as a key pressure signal that could change the meaning of GDP recovery.

This matters because recovery speed and recovery quality are not necessarily the same. A fast rebound may still come with inflation pressure that creates a more fragile or less balanced recovery profile. By evaluating GDP growth together with inflation, the project aimed to move beyond headline recovery and toward a more careful reading of post-COVID performance.

## Why Cross-Country Comparison Was Useful

A single-country analysis would have shown trend movement, but it would have been harder to judge whether the observed pattern was strong, weak, unusual, or common. Cross-country comparison adds contrast.

The project focused on China, France, Germany, Singapore, and the United States. These countries were not selected to represent the entire global economy. They were selected to create a focused comparison across major economies with different post-COVID paths, policy environments, and inflation responses.

That contrast made it easier to test whether recovery speed and inflation pressure moved together in similar or different ways across countries.

## Why the Data Had to Be Restructured

The source data came from public World Bank datasets. In raw form, the files were not analysis-ready for this comparison. The year values were arranged in wide format, which made it difficult to compare GDP growth and inflation consistently across the same country-year structure.

To make the comparison valid, the data had to be cleaned, reduced to the relevant years, reshaped into long format, and then joined into one country-year table. This was an important analytical step, not just a technical cleanup step.

Without that restructuring, the charts might still have looked polished, but the comparison would have been much less reliable.

## What the Final Comparison Was Trying to Test

The final comparison was built to test one main idea: whether post-COVID recovery still looked equally strong once GDP rebound was viewed together with inflation.

The GDP trend chart showed the depth of the 2020 contraction and the pace of rebound that followed. The inflation trend chart added a second layer, showing whether that rebound occurred alongside rising price pressure. The combined relationship view was used to examine how growth and inflation sat together rather than as isolated trends.

Taken together, the analysis was designed to compare recovery speed against recovery quality rather than reward GDP rebound alone.

## What the Analysis Suggests

The project suggests that headline recovery can be misleading when interpreted through GDP alone. Several economies showed visible rebound after the 2020 shock, but that rebound did not always appear equally strong once inflation was included in the picture.

This does not mean GDP recovery is unimportant. It means GDP recovery becomes more informative when interpreted with a second pressure signal. In that sense, the project supports a more cautious reading of post-COVID strength.

## What the Analysis Cannot Conclude

This project is descriptive and comparative. It does not establish causal economic relationships. It does not claim inflation alone defines stability, and it does not attempt to measure every dimension of recovery quality.

The analysis is also limited to two indicators, five countries, and the 2018 to 2023 period. The value of the project is not that it provides a complete macroeconomic model. The value is that it shows how a more careful analytical lens can change the interpretation of a headline metric.
